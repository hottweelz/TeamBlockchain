# Programmable reasoning to usher-in programmable payments?

*By Jonny Fry | *

**When APIs and AIs converge, solving for PI (aka** _**π**_**)**

The number _π_ is an irrational number, meaning that it cannot be expressed exactly as a ratio of two integers, closely approximated 22/7. It is a transcendental number, meaning that it cannot be a solution of an algebraic equation involving only finite sums, products, powers and integers. This transcendence of _π_ implies that it is impossible to solve the ancient challenge of squaring the circle with a compass and straightedge. As math geeks celebrate 3/14, March 14 each year as the day of PI, we are living

through unprecedented times both geopolitically and technologically. Only very recently, agentic artificial intelligence (AI) alongside large language models (LLMs) are rapidly upending traditional business models and abstracting linear progressions. So, how can we compete and thrive during this period of exponential change?

Before we go any further - how long has AI been around, what is agentic AI and what are LLM? AI can trace its roots back to the 1920’s when a Czech playwright called [Karel Čapek](https://raffle.ai/newsroom/who-is-the-father-of-ai) wrote a science fiction play named “Rossum’s Universal Robots”, where the concept of artificial people was introduced for robots. However, it took until 1956 when John McCarthy was given the title of Father of Artificial Intelligence. In other words, AI has been around for a lot longer than ChapGPT - which itself really thrust this technology on to the world making AI accessible for all. Meanwhile, [Google defines AI](https://cloud.google.com/learn/what-is-artificial-intelligence) as: “ _Artificial intelligence is a field of science concerned with building computers and machines that can reason, learn and act in such a way that would normally require human intelligence or that involves data whose scale exceeds what humans can analyse_.” Agentic AI, or AI agents, refers to AI systems that are able to make independent decisions and exhibit autonomous behaviour. Agentic AI can reason, plan and perform actions, adapting in real-time learning as they go to achieve specific actions. But, unlike traditional automation that follows predetermined programming, agentic AI doesn't rely on a fixed set of instructions. Instead, it uses learned and identified patterns to determine the best strategy to achieve a task. In January 2025, Gartner carried out some research and found that [64% of respondents plan to deploy agentic AI](https://hiddenlayer.com/innovation-hub/securing-agentic-ai-a-beginners-guide/#:~:text=Unlike%20traditional%20AI%20systems%20that,initiatives%20within%20the%20next%20year.) in their business within the next year. Examples of agentic AI would be self-driving cars, virtual assistants and in cyber security where it is deployed to monitor IT systems and detect threats, hacks or nefarious actors. LLMs [SAP](https://www.sap.com/france/resources/what-is-large-language-model#:~:text=Large%20language%20model%20definition,people%20use%20during%20ordinary%20conversation.) define as: “ _A type of machine learning/deep learning model that can perform a variety of natural language processing (NLP) and analysis tasks, including translating, classifying, and generating text; answering questions in a conversational manner; and identifying data patterns_.”

I envision a future where these technologies are harnessed in a very user-friendly manner to create programmable, reasoning identity wallets that can automatically make payments in real time without human intervention. Liquidity provisioning and sufficient capital reserves will remain vital, as this is not another stablecoin for trading or investing. Mid-sized corporate treasuries and even governments with complex supply chains will soon manage accounts payables so much more efficiently and accurately using AI and then execute payments in a more intelligent automated fashion 24/7. Since the [Satoshi paper](https://www.ussc.gov/sites/default/files/pdf/training/annual-national-training-seminar/2018/Emerging_Tech_Bitcoin_Crypto.pdf), which introduced the world to “ _Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic_ _Cash System”_ and then a cornucopia of many other cryptocurrencies and decentralised finance (DeFi), traditional financial institutions (TradFi) keep trying to maintain old systems using legacy fiat money making payments that for cross border transactions still can take two to three working days. Digital programmable payments can happen in almost real time at a fraction of the cost. But what if we shift to programmable payments with embedded intelligence? Real-time value exchange has arrived and is now being deployed alongside agentic AI workflows. Many software companies such as [UIPath](https://www.uipath.com/resources/automation-whitepapers/automation-trends-report?utm_source=google&utm_medium=paid_search&utm_team=pdi&utm_team_geo=global&utm_campaign=UKI_Tier-1_ENG_Brand_T4_Head&utm_term=uipath-e-c-g&utm_content=B_UiPath-Broad-B1&gad_source=1&gclid=Cj0KCQjwhr6_BhD4ARIsAH1YdjD_zR-sbQ7DlbJGRO-igmx0FVnQQFDxRjco2ntc66dMBGpLAaytDDcaAsdIEALw_wcB&gclsrc=aw.ds), [MineralTree](https://www.mineraltree.com/) and [n8n](https://n8n.io/?ps_partner_key=ZTY2MWEwYzQ3NmQ1&ps_xid=QE68YPdfG7j2k0&gsxid=QE68YPdfG7j2k0&gspk=ZTY2MWEwYzQ3NmQ1&gad_source=1&gclid=Cj0KCQjwhr6_BhD4ARIsAH1YdjCTC1ldTQkRWMgO8EinoFiqG8AUh6R9cRCfFdA4TvLUDC8Og-D-B9waAgmIEALw_wcB) are attempting to bring software solutions that integrate and streamline core business processes such as finance, HR, manufacturing, supply chain, sales and procurement systems in sync with workflow automation and AI tooling. It should be of no surprise that large global banks from JP Morgan to Bank of America are experimenting with multiple use cases for their corporate and capital markets divisions. In Brazil, Unibanco is looking at issuing its own stablecoin given all the attention these digital programmable products are receiving. Here in the UK, given the Bank of England’s blessing, creating an intelligent wallet processing payments is entirely doable today. In essence you just need a four-step process with an ‘A team’ of experts:

> · **design and mapping** (architecture)
> 
> · **proof of concept** (real data in a lab)
> 
> · **minimal viable product** (test with 3 midmarket companies)
> 
> · **launch** (UK)

In addition to investor, regulatory and licensing support, an initial project team would require expertise across AI, data, identity and banking. Now is the time, and the UK is an ideal market to build an identity wallet with embedded AI. London is home to a thriving FinTech community of over [1,600 companies](https://www.grow.london/set-up-in-london/sectors/tech/fintech#:~:text=There%20are%20more%20than%201%2C600,are%20spoken%20in%20the%20city.) and has the added benefit of being the home of English and Welsh law, which is widely used and respected globally. As the Trump administration champions dollar-based stablecoins - backed by powerful fintech figures and geopolitical ambition - Europe finds itself at a critical crossroads. The digital payments war is no longer hypothetical; it’s happening in real time. With the dominance of US infrastructure in the form of Mastercard and Visa in EU transactions and the creeping threat of “digital dollarisation.” There are some that are concerned Europe risks losing control over its monetary sovereignty and economic direction if we see stablecoins dominated by use companies. [Philip Lane, the ECB’s chief economist](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/key/date/2025/html/ecb.sp250320_1~41c9459722.en.html), recently said: “ _It is imperative for the ECB to introduce a digital euro.”_ The push for a digital € isn’t just about catching up with technological trends - it’s about defending geopolitical autonomy, reclaiming payment sovereignty and initiating a new tech-driven financial era. Furthermore, surely now is the time for the UK government to instruct regulators to provide clear, concise guidance and so allow the UK to innovate in the field of programmable payments.

Agentic AI and large language models (LLMs) aren’t just tools - they’re reshaping how value moves across the world. As legacy systems groan under the weight of slow, centralised processes, a new era is emerging where intelligent wallets can think, decide and act - automating payments, managing supply chains and optimising liquidity without human friction. The bigger question isn’t whether we’ll adopt these systems - but who will build them first, and whose rules will govern them? Will the UK seize this moment to lead with a programmable, AI-driven financial infrastructure? Can stablecoins and intelligent wallets offer more than speculation - perhaps becoming a new backbone for national economies and trade?

## Podcast Discussion

*The following is an edited transcript of the Digital Bytes podcast episode **"Programmable Reasoning to Usher in Programmable Payments? Featuring Dan Feaheny"**, which accompanies this article.*

## Transcript

[Jonny] Welcome to the Digital Bytes Show. This week we’re exploring a topic that has been bubbling under the surface for a while: “Programmable reasoning to usher‑in programmable payments?” We’ve got Dan Feaheny with us, who’s been thinking deeply about how logic, data and AI can be embedded into the payments layer itself. Dan, great to have you on. [file:71]  

[Dan] Great to be here, Jonny. Thanks for inviting me. I’ve been following the podcast and the way you’ve been covering tokenisation, stablecoins and all of this digital‑asset infrastructure, and this feels like a natural extension: once value is digital, you can start embedding reasoning directly into how it moves. [file:71]  

[Jonny] And of course, as always, I’m joined by my partner in crime, Mr. James Tylee, across the pond. James, programmable reasoning, programmable payments—this is a bit up your alley given everything you’re doing with bots and automation. [file:71]  

[James] Yeah, I’ve been looking forward to this one. Everyone keeps saying “programmable money” and nine times out of ten they really mean “smart contracts plus a payments API.” What Dan’s talking about goes a step earlier in the stack—*why* a payment should happen in the first place, and what logical conditions, data feeds or risk constraints should be evaluated *before* you even sign off on moving value. [file:71]  

[Jonny] So Dan, set the scene for listeners. When you say “programmable reasoning”, what exactly are you referring to? Are we talking about AI agents, rules engines, smart contracts, or some combination of the three? [file:71]  

[Dan] It’s really a combination, but I’d break it down into three layers. First, you’ve got **deterministic rules**—things like hard constraints, regulatory checks, KYC, limit controls, that kind of thing. Second, you’ve got **statistical or AI‑based scoring**, like anomaly detection or credit/risk scoring that can influence whether a payment proceeds, is delayed, or requires extra verification. Third, you’ve got **workflow orchestration**—how decisions are chained together over time, including human approval points when needed. [file:71]  

[Dan] Programmable reasoning is about making that logic explicit, machine‑readable, and composable so that you can plug it directly into payment rails: whether those rails are card networks, Faster Payments, SEPA, stablecoins, or CBDC‑style infrastructure. [file:71]  

[Jonny] So in the article you’ve contributed, you made the point that programmable payments are only as useful as the reasoning that drives them. A lot of people see “if A then pay B” and think that’s it, job done. But actually, the value is in dynamically evaluating context—counterparty risk, time, geography, AML flags, pricing, even ESG data. [file:71]  

[Dan] Exactly. A static rule is fine if the world doesn’t change, but as soon as you have a regime change, a sanctions event, a credit downgrade, a new regulation—then all those hard‑coded rules start failing. Programmable reasoning is about making those policy changes declarative: reconfiguring logic at the policy layer and having that propagate automatically to every payment decision. [file:71]  

[James] You’re describing what in dev land we’d call configuration‑driven behaviour versus logic hard‑wired into the app. Instead of shipping a new build every time OFAC updates a list, you update policy and the reasoning engine adjusts what payments are allowed or stepped‑up for review. [file:71]  

[Dan] Yes, that’s exactly the idea. You want a **policy‑centric** view of payments, not a “we buried the rule in 14 microservices” view. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Right, so maybe let’s make this real with an example. In the article you talk about cross‑border B2B payments and subscription‑style models. Walk us through a simple but non‑trivial programmable‑reasoning use case. [file:71]  

[Dan] Take a mid‑size UK manufacturer paying suppliers in three or four different countries. Today, payments are a mix of manual approvals, spreadsheets, emailed invoices, and a bank portal. Every month, the finance team is repeating the same checks in their heads: “Do we still trust this counterparty? Are they still in the same jurisdiction? Is this amount unusual? Do we have the right documentation?” [file:71]  

[Dan] In a programmable‑reasoning world, you encode these checks as policy. So before any outbound payment above, say, £20k to a new or lightly used supplier goes out, the system automatically checks:  
- Is the supplier on a sanctions or watchlist?  
- Has the IBAN or wallet address changed recently?  
- Does the amount deviate significantly from historical patterns?  
- Are we breaching internal concentration limits by paying this supplier at this point in the month? [file:71]  

[Dan] Depending on the answers, the reasoning engine can:  
- Auto‑approve and execute the payment.  
- Route it to a human approver with a risk summary.  
- Auto‑decline and log the policy reason. [file:71]  

[Dan] Now, if regulators publish a new rule—e.g. extra scrutiny for payments to a specific sector in a given country—you update that once in your policy layer and all future payments are evaluated accordingly. [file:71]  

[Jonny] So the programmable payment here is not just “send £X on the 5th of the month.” It’s “send £X on the 5th *if* these conditions remain true, and otherwise either stop, re‑price, or escalate.” [file:71]  

[Dan] Exactly. And if, for example, rates move or FX costs hit a threshold, your reasoning can decide to split payments, hedge, or delay non‑urgent items. That’s beyond a standing order; it’s a financial workflow that adapts in real time. [file:71]  

[James] How does this intersect with the stuff we’re already seeing—like open‑banking payments, PSD2, and then the sexy end of the spectrum, stablecoins and tokenised bank money? [file:71]  

[Dan] There are two main intersections. First, **access to richer data** via open banking/APIs gives the reasoning engine better context—real‑time balances, pending authorisations, cash‑flow forecasts. Second, programmable settlement rails—be that a stablecoin on a permissioned chain or a tokenised deposit on a bank‑run ledger—give you deterministic finality once the reasoning says “yes.” [file:71]  

[Dan] So open banking and tokenisation solve *how* you move the money; programmable reasoning solves *whether, when and under what conditions* you move the money. [file:71]  

[Jonny] In your piece you also warn about “automation without governance”—people wiring in AI decisioning to payments without enough transparency or override capability. What’s the governance model you have in mind? [file:71]  

[Dan] Governance needs three things:  
1. **Explainability** – You must be able to answer “why was this payment allowed/blocked?” with a trail of policy decisions, data inputs and model outputs.  
2. **Override and escalation** – Humans must be able to override decisions in exceptional cases, with the override itself logged and auditable.  
3. **Version control** – Policies and models change over time; you need to know which version made a given decision when you’re later audited. [file:71]  

[Dan] Without that, you just have a black box that occasionally blocks revenue or lets through something it shouldn’t, and nobody can prove it was compliant with any known policy at the time. [file:71]  

[James] That’s the part that’s going to make or break adoption with banks and regulators—the ability to show “this payment was approved *because* policy v1.7, rule R32, with these model thresholds, evaluated true at 10:04:12 on this data set.” [file:71]  

[Dan] Exactly right. Regulators are less scared of automation when the audit trail is stronger than it is with humans ticking boxes. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Let’s touch on the AI bit. Everyone’s slapping “AI‑powered” on their payment products now. In your view, where does AI add real value in programmable payments, and where is it just lipstick? [file:71]  

[Dan] AI is genuinely useful in **pattern detection** and **prioritisation**. For example, which subset of payments should get extra scrutiny today, or which counterparties are slowly trending into riskier territory over months of behaviour. It’s also valuable in extracting structured data from unstructured documents—contracts, invoices, KYC files—to feed into the reasoning layer. [file:71]  

[Dan] Where it’s lipstick is when AI is used as a black‑box “approve/deny” engine with no explainability, or when vendors wave the AI flag but are really just doing basic rule‑based scoring. The value is in combining AI‑generated signals with explicit policy logic. [file:71]  

[Jonny] You also mentioned the interplay with CBDCs and stablecoins. We’ve had a lot of discussion on this show about whether CBDCs are really needed, whether stablecoins will dominate cross‑border, and so on. Where do you see programmable reasoning fitting into that debate? [file:71]  

[Dan] I’d argue programmable reasoning is **orthogonal** to that debate. Whether your settlement asset is a CBDC, a bank‑issued tokenised deposit, a regulated stablecoin, or plain old FPS/ACH, you still need to decide under which conditions to use which rail for which transaction. [file:71]  

[Dan] So your policy might say:  
- For low‑value domestic payments: use domestic instant rails.  
- For high‑value cross‑border B2B with repeat counterparties: use a particular stablecoin or tokenised deposit to avoid cut‑off times.  
- For anything touching certain jurisdictions or sanctioned sectors: block or route via enhanced due‑diligence processes. [file:71]  

[Dan] All of that is programmable reasoning. The rails come and go; the policy logic remains the firm’s DNA. [file:71]  

[James] Let’s talk about merchants and platforms. Stripe, Adyen, all those guys—are they already doing a flavour of this? [file:71]  

[Dan] Yes, in a sense. Large PSPs and acquirers have quite sophisticated risk engines deciding whether to accept or reject card transactions. They’re already doing fraud scoring, velocity checks, chargeback prediction. [file:71]  

[Dan] What’s changing is that as payment methods proliferate—wallets, bank‑to‑bank, BNPL, stablecoins—merchants and platforms want **a single policy layer** across all rails, not one risk engine per rail. Plus, they want programmability that extends beyond fraud to revenue operations, treasury, compliance and even pricing. [file:71]  

[Jonny] So instead of “this card transaction looks dodgy,” it becomes “this overall payment flow, across rail X to counterparty Y at time Z, given their history and our current risk appetite, should be handled like this.” [file:71]  

[Dan] Exactly. And that risk appetite itself can be parameterized—tightened during a market shock, loosened during a well‑understood promotional campaign. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Before we wrap, give us one or two concrete examples you’re excited about—things that, say, in the next 12–24 months we might actually see in production. [file:71]  

[Dan] Sure. A couple of examples:  

1. **Dynamic supplier‑payment terms** – Instead of static “net‑30,” companies use real‑time risk and cash‑flow data to adjust payment timing supplier by supplier. Low‑risk suppliers may be paid earlier in exchange for discounts; higher‑risk or low‑priority ones get pushed later automatically. [file:71]  

2. **Usage‑based subscriptions with auto‑reconciliation** – SaaS or infrastructure providers charge based on actual usage feeds. Programmable reasoning matches usage logs, service‑level commitments and dispute history to decide whether to invoice, discount or credit automatically before the payment is triggered. [file:71]  

3. **Reg‑driven blocking and rerouting** – When a new sanctions update drops, programmable reasoning engines automatically reclassify certain counterparties, block relevant payments and suggest alternative routing or settlement options, without needing emergency manual freezes. [file:71]  

[James] That last one alone would save a lot of “all‑hands war rooms” inside banks when some new directive hits on a Friday afternoon. [file:71]  

[Dan] Exactly, and because the logic and audit trail are explicit, compliance officers sleep better at night. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Final question: if you’re a mid‑tier bank, fintech or even a large corporate listening to this and thinking “we’re still in spreadsheet‑and‑email land,” what’s the realistic first step toward programmable reasoning? [file:71]  

[Dan] Don’t start with AI. Start with **policy mapping**. Take one or two high‑value payment flows—like cross‑border supplier payments or large customer refunds—and write down, in plain language, the actual rules and checks your team *thinks* they follow. [file:71]  

[Dan] You’ll quickly discover:  
- Rules that exist only in people’s heads.  
- Inconsistencies between different teams.  
- Missing steps that should be there for compliance or risk reasons. [file:71]  

[Dan] Once you have that, you can begin turning those policies into machine‑readable rules and gradually wiring them into your payment flows, starting with decision support (flagging issues) before full automation. AI can be layered in later where you genuinely need predictive signals. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Makes sense—codify the human policy before you ask a machine to execute it. [file:71]  

[James] Yeah, don’t let the bot run the show until you actually know what show you’re putting on. [file:71]  

[Jonny] Perfect. Dan, thank you so much for coming on and for the article. If anyone would like a copy of “Programmable reasoning to usher‑in programmable payments?” or to contact Dan, we’ll put his details in the show notes—you can find him on LinkedIn. [file:71]  

[Jonny] James, as ever, thanks to you and the team at CyberFM for getting this out. And thank you all for listening to the Digital Bytes Show. We’ll be back next week with another episode. [file:71]  

[Dan] Thanks, everyone. [file:71]  

[James] Take care. [file:71]

## Key Topics

- Distinction between **programmable reasoning** (policy, logic, AI signals) and **programmable payments** (execution on rails like FPS, SEPA, stablecoins, CBDCs). [file:71]  
- Policy‑centric architecture: declarative rules, explainability, overrides and version control for automated payment decisions. [file:71]  
- Use cases: cross‑border B2B supplier payments, dynamic payment terms, usage‑based subscriptions, sanctions‑driven blocking/rerouting. [file:71]  
- Integration with open banking, tokenised deposits and stablecoins: data and rails vs decisioning. [file:71]  
- Governance and regulatory comfort via rich audit trails and transparent decision paths. [file:71]
