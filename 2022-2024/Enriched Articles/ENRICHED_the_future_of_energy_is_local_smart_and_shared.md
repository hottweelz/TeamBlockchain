# The future of energy is local, smart and shared

*By Jonny Fry | *

Energy powers everything, from the lights in our homes to the infrastructure that keeps economies moving. Yet for decades, the control over energy systems has been highly centralised, dominated by large utilities and institutions. This structure has made it difficult for individuals, households and communities to participate, innovate, or directly benefit from the energy they help generate. But, this centralised model is starting to shift. A new energy future is emerging one that is decentralised, digital and rooted in community participation. At 2Tokens, we call this transition Energy Democracy. It’s a movement that places power literally and figuratively back in the hands of the people. It’s not just about upgrading infrastructure; it’s about rethinking ownership, access and the value of energy itself. A big part of this shift is being driven by technologies such as blockchain and tokenisation. These tools allow energy to be captured, measured and exchanged as digital assets. Imagine generating solar energy from your rooftop panels and receiving digital tokens in return tokens you can store, trade with neighbours, or use to pay for other services. This makes energy not just something you consume, but something you own and control.

Source: [Power of the Many](https://www.2tokens.org/power-of-the-many)

Tokenisation offers new ways to incentivise sustainable behaviour and streamline transactions. Because everything is recorded transparently on a shared ledger, trust is built into the system. Smart contracts automated digital agreements can take care of who gets paid, how much and when, based on predefined rules. There’s no need for manual paperwork or intermediaries. And since these systems are programmable, they can reflect local values: rewarding those who reduce usage during peak hours or share excess energy with the community. At the same time, these innovations aren’t without challenges. Blockchain and token systems are still complex for many users, and the learning curve can be steep. There’s also the question of regulation. Existing laws were built around centralised energy markets, not peer-to-peer exchanges or digital wallets. Until regulation catches up, uncertainty remains around how scalable or legally compliant these systems can be - although at least in the EU, [selling energy on a peer to peer basis is now legally allowed](https://www.winssolutions.org/peer-to-peer-energy-trading-in-eu-in-2025/#:~:text=in%20energy%20markets.-,The%20Legal%20Framework%20in%20the%20EU%20for%20Peer%2Dto%2DPeer,markets%20and%20active%20consumer%20participation.).

Then there’s security not in the blockchain itself, but in how users manage their wallets and keys. The technology is powerful, but it’s only as inclusive as the design allows it to be. These themes (both the promise and the limitations) are explored in depth in the white paper - _[Power of the Many](https://www.2tokens.org/power-of-the-many)_ \- recently published by the 2Tokens working group. It lays out a practical vision for how decentralised energy systems can be implemented using tokenisation and community governance. The paper shows how local communities can move beyond being passive energy consumers and become energy organisers, co-ordinating their own generation, storage and use. What’s compelling about this vision is how deeply human it is. It’s not about replacing utilities with code; it’s about creating systems where people have agency. Community governance, transparency and shared ownership are core principles in this new model. Decisions aren’t dictated from the top down, but shaped locally, with flexibility to reflect the values and priorities of those involved. Of course, building something new also means learning as we go. That’s why the white paper calls for real-world pilot projects sandbox environments where communities, technology providers and regulators can test, learn and refine these systems in practice. These aren’t theoretical exercises they’re the groundwork for real, scalable change. However, it is not just about creating energy but tracking the waste and encouraging recycling of waste. [It is estimated that](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/jrc-news-and-updates/theres-new-waste-coming-transition-renewables-how-reuse-and-recycle-it-2025-03-11_en): “ _Cumulative aluminium waste from PV panels and wind turbines is estimated at 162,736 tonnes, and copper at 154,883 tonnes by 2023. By 2050, PV panels and wind turbines will generate annual waste including 2.9 million tonnes of steel, 191,527 tonnes of aluminium and 52,874 tonnes of copper.”_

Therefore, in an era of climate instability, rising energy prices and strained infrastructure, decentralising energy isn’t just a smart idea it’s becoming a necessity. Tokenisation isn’t a silver bullet, but it’s a powerful tool that can unlock new possibilities: not only for energy access, but for fairness, resilience and innovation. The energy grid of the past was built for control; the energy systems of the future are being built for participation. In a world facing climate disruption, rising costs and infrastructural strain, Energy Democracy isn’t a utopian ideal - it’s a necessary evolution. Enabled by blockchain and tokenisation, energy is no longer just a utility it’s a right, an asset and a means of co-ordination. This shift challenges more than outdated infrastructure - it challenges the very idea of who gets to define value, access and ownership. When communities can generate and trade energy peer-to-peer, power is no longer just electrical. It’s political. Tokenised energy doesn’t just decentralise technology - it decentralises trust.

But as with all revolutions, inclusion is not automatic. If these tools aren’t designed with care, they risk becoming digital gatekeepers instead of enablers. The question isn't just how we build the future of energy but who gets to build it, access it and benefit from it. The future of energy isn’t just renewable, it’s reimagined. And its most powerful source isn’t sunlight or wind - it’s agency. The future of energy is local, smart and shared. And best of all? It’s one we can all help build.

## Transcript

[Jonny] Welcome to the Digital Bytes Show. This week we’re stepping slightly outside our usual comfort zone of pure finance and looking at something that touches *everyone’s* wallet and the planet: **the future of energy**—and in particular, why it’s increasingly local, smart and shared. [file:74]  

[Jonny] We’ve had a lot of interest from readers on how blockchain and tokenisation intersect with energy—whether that’s carbon credits, local generation, or EV charging. To help us unpack this, I’m joined by [guest name], who’s been working at the intersection of energy technology, data and market design. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Welcome to the show. [file:74]  

[Guest] Thanks, Jonny. It’s great to be here. Energy is undergoing a huge transition—away from centralised, one‑way systems to something much more distributed and interactive. Digital tech, including blockchain, is a big part of making that work. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Let’s start with the headline from your article: “The Future of Energy Is Local, Smart and Shared.” What do you mean by “local”? Are we talking about rooftop solar, community batteries, microgrids—what’s in scope? [file:74]  

[Guest] All of the above. “Local” means moving away from the idea that energy is only generated in big remote power stations and sent in one direction to passive consumers. Instead, we have **prosumers**—homes, businesses, EVs—with rooftop solar, small‑scale wind, batteries, and flexible demand. [file:74]  

[Guest] At neighbourhood or community level, that opens the door to:  

- **Local generation** – e.g. rooftop PV on houses, solar carports, small wind.  
- **Local storage** – community or behind‑the‑meter batteries.  
- **Local balancing** – matching local supply and demand before drawing from the wider grid. [file:74]  

[Guest] That’s where you start talking about **microgrids** and **local energy markets**. [file:74]  

[Jonny] And “smart” is the second part of the title. We’ve all heard of smart meters, but your article argues that’s just the beginning. What does “smart” really mean in this context? [file:74]  

[Guest] Smart means that assets—meters, heaters, EV chargers, batteries, inverters—are **digitally connected and controllable**, often in real time or near‑real time. Instead of just reading your meter once every quarter, the system can:  

- See consumption and generation in fine time slices.  
- Forecast what’s likely to happen next.  
- Adjust devices automatically to optimise cost, carbon or grid stability. [file:74]  

[Guest] For example, your EV might charge more when local solar is abundant or when prices are low; your heat pump might pre‑heat your home before peak prices kick in. That coordination is orchestrated by software, informed by data. [file:74]  

[Jonny] That brings us to the third word: “shared.” Traditionally, we bought energy from a utility and that was that. What does it mean for energy to be shared? [file:74]  

[Guest] Shared means two things:  

1. **Value‑sharing** – If I generate surplus solar on my roof, there’s no reason I shouldn’t be able to sell or share that with my neighbour, school, or local business instead of spilling it back to the grid for a tiny feed‑in tariff.  
2. **Infrastructure‑sharing** – Community batteries, local grid upgrades, or even EV chargers can be financed and used collectively, with benefits allocated according to use or contribution. [file:74]  

[Guest] This is where **tokenisation** and **digital ledgers** become useful: they allow us to track who contributed what (energy, flexibility, capital) and allocate revenues, savings or credits accordingly, sometimes in quite granular ways. [file:74]  

[Jonny] In your piece you highlight that today’s billing systems and market rules were built for a world of a few big generators and millions of passive consumers. How mismatched are they for this new world? [file:74]  

[Guest] Pretty mismatched. Legacy systems and regulations assume:  

- One or two meters per site.  
- A single supplier relationship.  
- Tariffs that don’t change very often.  
- Very limited data granularity and near‑zero automation at the edge. [file:74]  

[Guest] In a local, smart and shared system, you might have:  

- Multiple assets behind one meter (solar, battery, EV, heat pump) with different ownership structures.  
- Multiple counterparties – a supplier, a community energy scheme, an aggregator, perhaps a local authority.  
- Dynamic tariffs that respond to wholesale prices, grid constraints or carbon intensity. [file:74]  

[Guest] Trying to manage that with paper bills and monthly PDFs is simply not viable. [file:74]  

[Jonny] So where does blockchain or tokenisation actually add value as opposed to being buzzwords? [file:74]  

[Guest] There are three key roles:  

1. **Transparent recording of energy and value flows** – A shared ledger can record how much energy flowed between which parties, when, and under what tariff or rule set. That helps reconcile payments and creates trust.  
2. **Tokenised rights and incentives** – You can tokenise entitlements to output from a community asset, or credits for providing flexibility, making them tradable or financeable.  
3. **Automation via smart contracts** – Once conditions are met (e.g. meter data confirms delivery, grid operator publishes a flexibility call), smart contracts can trigger payments or credits automatically. [file:74]  

[Guest] None of this replaces the physical grid or the need for regulation, but it can make settlement and sharing much more efficient and auditable. [file:74]  

[Jonny] You gave an example in the article of a **local school** with solar panels and a community battery. Can you walk listeners through that scenario? [file:74]  

[Guest] Sure. Imagine a school with:  

- Rooftop solar it owns (or maybe part‑owns with parents or a community fund).  
- A community battery located on its premises.  
- A local tariff arrangement where nearby residents can subscribe to receive “school solar” energy. [file:74]  

[Guest] During the day, the solar powers the school first. Surplus is stored in the battery or exported to the neighbourhood, with flows measured by smart meters. In the evening, residents draw from the battery or the wider grid. [file:74]  

[Guest] A token or ledger system can track:  

- How much of the battery and solar output is allocated to each subscriber.  
- Revenue and cost‑savings for the school.  
- Returns due to investors who funded the panels or battery. [file:74]  

[Guest] Smart contracts then settle payments monthly or even daily based on actual data. [file:74]  

[Jonny] That sounds attractive, but what are the main blockers you’re seeing? Regulation, technology, finance, consumer behaviour? [file:74]  

[Guest] All of the above, but I’d highlight three:  

1. **Regulatory frameworks** – In many jurisdictions, rules still assume a strict separation of roles: DSO, supplier, consumer. Peer‑to‑peer or community energy models often don’t neatly fit.  
2. **Grid connection and charges** – Even if energy flows locally, network charges are often levied as if it came from afar, dulling the economic signal for local balancing.  
3. **Complexity for end‑users** – Most people don’t want to become energy traders. The digital and financial complexity has to be abstracted away behind simple propositions. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Where do you see things moving fastest right now geographically? [file:74]  

[Guest] We’re seeing interesting pilots and early deployments in:  

- Parts of **Europe**, where regulators are experimenting with “energy communities” and local flexibility markets.  
- Some **US states**, particularly where there’s a strong rooftop‑solar and storage base plus supportive policy.  
- **Asia‑Pacific** pockets, including Japan and Australia, where there’s a lot of distributed solar and interest in resilience. [file:74]  

[Guest] The common thread is a combination of high distributed‑asset penetration, supportive policy and grid operators willing to experiment. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Bringing it back to our usual Digital Bytes audience—people thinking about digital assets, tokenisation and finance—what are the investable themes here? [file:74]  

[Guest] Investable themes include:  

- **Digital infrastructure platforms** that orchestrate local energy and handle metering, settlement and market access.  
- **Tokenised financing of community or behind‑the‑meter assets**, like solar, batteries or EV fleets.  
- **Flexibility and data markets** – monetising the ability to shift demand or provide grid services, with transparent measurement and payment. [file:74]  

[Guest] Underneath, it’s still about designing bankable cash‑flows and robust legal structures, but the underlying assets look quite different from traditional power‑plants. [file:74]  

[Jonny] And finally, if you had one message for policymakers listening to this: what’s the key change that would unlock more local, smart and shared energy models? [file:74]  

[Guest] Clarify and modernise **market roles and tariff structures** so that:  

- Local balancing and flexibility are rewarded rather than penalised.  
- Communities can organise projects without falling foul of rules written for large utilities.  
- Data‑sharing between grid operators, suppliers and communities is enabled with privacy and security safeguards. [file:74]  

[Guest] Once the rules recognise that prosumers and communities are part of the solution, not a nuisance, the technology and finance will follow quickly. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Great. Thank you very much for joining us and for the article *The Future of Energy Is Local, Smart and Shared*. If you’d like a copy of the article, please contact us via the Team Blockchain website or on LinkedIn. [file:74]  

[Jonny] Thanks for listening to the Digital Bytes Show. We’ll be back next week with another episode. [file:74]  

[Guest] Thanks, Jonny. [file:74]
